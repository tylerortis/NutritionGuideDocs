Introduction

Give this guide a thorough reading without stopping. You'll get the big picture, and then you'll be able to come back to it with new eyes, ready to delve in and apply it to your objectives and life.

What Are Calories?

Before we talk about macronutrients, aka “macros”, let’s answer the question: what is a calorie? A calorie is the amount of energy required to raise 1 gram of water by 1 degree Celsius. As it relates to fitness and nutrition, it’s easier to think of a calorie as a measurement of the energy we need to fuel our bodies’ functional systems.

For those of you who have “counted calories” before, you may have found yourself losing weight, maybe even reaching your goal weight, but not coming anywhere close to your ideal physique.

Here’s why:

If you eat less calories than you burn, you will lose weight, according to the simple calories in, calories out equation. Even though this is true, our bodies are more than just energy in and energy out. You'll learn more about your nutrition, body, and mentality by eating the appropriate kinds of calories (yes, there are multiple kinds!) Not only will you achieve fantastic results, but you'll also learn more about your nutrition, body, and mindset than you ever imagined.

Calories are a good place to start, but looking beyond them is the key to achieving your personal goals. Building muscle, having energy all day, feeling energized and strong for workouts, and truly getting rid of any food anxiety or guilt are just a few of the fantastic benefits that come from a well-rounded diet.

This is really where tracking your macronutrients comes into play…

What Are Macros?

In a nutshell, macros are the building blocks of calories. The three macronutrients are carbohydrates, proteins and fats and they each serve a different and important role in your body. Just to be sure we're all on the same page...

There are 4 calories in every gram of carbohydrate, 4 calories in every gram of protein and 9 calories in every gram of fat.

1g Carb = 4 Calories

1g Protein = 4 Calories

1g Fat = 9 Calories

Each macronutrient plays a specific role in your body. We need to look beyond calories so we don’t miss out on some of the things that are beneficial for our overall health, wellness and performance.

Let’s break down each of the macronutrients, starting with Protein.

Protein

What Does it Do?

Protein is found in every part of your body including your organs, tissues, muscles and even your hormones. Here are a few great things to know about protein:

  • Protein has the highest thermic effect of food, which is a fancy way of saying that it uses the most energy to digest.
  • Protein supports lean muscle retention when your goal is to lose body fat and promotes muscle growth when you’re trying to get stronger.
  • Protein is made up of amino acids, which are essential for the building of muscle in the gym and the repairing of muscle on recovery days. The creation of protein from these amino acid building blocks is referred to as protein synthesis.
  • Protein keeps your body healthy, strong, and in a constant state of repair and renewal, allowing you to recover quickly from any physical effort.

Where can I find it?

There are plenty of other high-protein foods you can find right at your local grocery store:

  • Meats (steak, grass-fed beef, pork, game meat, poultry, eggs)
  • Seafood (salmon, tuna, white fish, scallops, shrimp)
  • Bone broth
  • Legumes (black beans, lentils, peas)
  • Dairy (Greek yogurt or kefir)
  • Tofu
  • Beans (black, kidney, pinto, navy)
  • Veggie/soy burgers
  • Protein powder

Not all protein is created equal. Figuring out which sources are lean enough to easily work into your macros every day and which should probably be eaten in moderation is a priority but can get confusing. Check out this list of protein sources categorized from lean to fatty.

Protein Isn’t Exclusive to Meat

Meat isn’t the only place you will find protein. Tons of delicious, high quality protein comes from plant-based proteins. Relying less on animal based proteins and more on vegetables will provide the added benefit of a higher volume diet with more micronutrients (aka vitamins and minerals).

These high-protein veggies will give you between 4-6 grams of protein per 200 grams:

  • Broccoli
  • Mushrooms
  • Corn
  • Spinach
  • Kale
  • Brussels sprouts
  • Artichoke
  • Cauliflower
  • Beet greens
  • Green beans
  • Potatoes
  • Arugula
  • Asparagus

Dairy products such as milk, yogurt, and cheese are also high in protein! Dairy products, like vegetables, are high in micronutrients such as calcium, vitamin D, phosphorus, potassium, magnesium, vitamins A, B12, and riboflavin. Some dairy products, like yogurt, include probiotics, which are beneficial to intestinal health.

A Quick Note on Protein Powders

When taken properly, protein supplements can be quite beneficial. They can be a terrific way to get your protein goal quickly and easily, but they shouldn't be your only source of protein. It's possible that you're hungry in between meals because you're drinking your protein rather than eating it. Chewing and swallowing your meal has been shown to have huge benefits!

PRO TIPS

  • Always have SOMETHING in the house that is exclusively protein like egg whites, whey or tuna.
  • If you have a hard time hitting your protein, get a head start by aiming to hit a third of your protein at breakfast.
  • If your protein target sounds overwhelming, divide it by the number of meals/snacks you're planning on having each day.
  • If you are low on protein you can add egg whites to almost anything to increase protein intake (both in liquid form and tiny pieces when hard boiled).
  • Mix whey protein in with iced or hot coffee for a rich and protein packed drink - trust us, it is delicious!

Carbohydrates

What Do They Do?

Carbohydrates, often known as carbs, are your body's main energy source. They are the fuel for your fire and the gas in your tank. Carbs are stored as glycogen in your muscles. Your brain and muscles will feel powerful and fresh if you eat carbs in the appropriate proportions! Here’s a few major things that are great about carbs:

  • When you’re training, carbs are the macronutrient that is most readily available for your body.
  • Carbohydrates replenish your depleted glycogen stores after a hard workout. This supports better performance and recovery for athletes involved in high-intensity training.

Let’s Talk About Dietary Fiber

Dietary fiber is a complex form of carbohydrates. There are two types of fiber soluble fiber and insoluble fiber. They are differentiated by their structure and the role they play in your body.

In your body, soluble fiber forms sticky, jelly-like substances. It is water soluble (it combines with water) and aids in the slow digestion and absorption of food, especially carbs. This is crucial for blood sugar regulation and avoiding the dreaded carb crash. Soluble fiber also aids in the reduction of blood cholesterol levels, which is beneficial to your heart!

Finally, we love soluble fiber because it keeps you full and guarantees you get the most out of your meal while you're on a low-calorie diet.

Some good sources of soluble fiber are:

  • Oatmeal
  • Barley
  • Beans
  • Nuts
  • Apples
  • Blueberries.

Insoluble fiber does not combine with water. That is, it aids in the bulking up of stool volume, the speeding up of food digestion, and the maintenance of a healthy digestive tract. Because insoluble fibers are present in plant cell walls, veggies are a wonderful way to increase your insoluble fiber intake. Whole grains include insoluble fiber as well.

Both are essential for intestinal health, illness prevention, and weight management, so striking a balance is critical. Fiber consumption should be around 10-15 grams per 1,000 calories as a general rule. You will automatically achieve a fantastic balance if you focus on consuming fiber through largely whole meals. Because high-fiber meals are high in micronutrients (vitamins and minerals), they are connected with a well-balanced diet.

Some of our favorite high fiber foods are:

Fats

What Do They Do?

Fats are the most calorically dense macronutrient (remember, 9 calories per gram), which was critical back in the "caveman days" when we didn't know when our next meal would arrive (see The Hungry Brain by Stephan Guyenet for more on this). Worrying about where your next meal will come from is no longer a problem thanks to our modern world, with stores and food delivery services accessible to almost everyone. The problem now is that our evolution hasn't completely caught up yet. We must be cautious about how much fat we consume because it is delicious and can improve the texture of most foods.  The trickiest thing about fats is that most fats are invisible in a cooked dish (think oil and butter), so you're probably eating way more than you realize.

Just like proteins and carbs, fats play an imperative role in our ability to survive and thrive. Here are just a few of the amazing things fat does in your body:

  • Fats insulate your body, protect your vital organs and assist with brain functionality.
  • Fats facilitate the transportation and absorption of the fat soluble vitamins A, D, E and K.
  • The omega-3 fatty acids EPA and DHA support cardiovascular health, joint health and digestion.
  • Fats slow down the digestion of your food which helps with satiety and keeps you fuller, longer!

Where Can I Find Healthy Fats?

  • Nuts
  • Seeds
  • Fatty fish like salmon and mackerel
  • Oils (olive, avocado, sesame)
  • Ghee
  • Avocado
  • Egg yolks

What Are Micronutrients?

Before we go any further, we want to be sure to list a few minor nutrients that are still important in your diet. Micronutrients are nutrients that your body needs in very small (or "micro") levels. Vitamins and minerals are popular names for micronutrients. There could be another whole guide about micronutrients, their functions in the body, and what happens if you don't receive enough of them, but here are a few key points to remember:

Vitamins

  • Vitamins are “essential,” which means that your body can’t make them fast enough to meet your daily needs.
  • Vitamins are present in very small amounts in foods.
  • It is important to get enough vitamins in your diet because they are needed for healthy body functions like growth and reproduction.
  • Some vitamins you may have heard of are Vitamin C, D, E, A, K and the B Vitamins (1-6, 9 and 12). These vitamins all play specific roles in your body.

Minerals

  • Most minerals are also “essential” and need to be consumed through diet.
  • Minerals can be broken down even further into “macrominerals” and “microminerals” depending on their daily requirements.
  • Minerals are present in very small amounts in foods.
  • Some minerals you may have heard of are calcium, phosphorus, potassium, magnesium, iron, zinc, fluoride and many more!

Vitamins and minerals can be found in most foods but whole foods like fruits, vegetables, nuts, seeds and whole grains are great places to start!
